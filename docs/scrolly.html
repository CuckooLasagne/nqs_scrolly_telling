Early Childhood Services in Victoria: Data Trends and Breakdown

This scrolly telling document outlines the performance of Victorian early childhood education and care services against the National Quality Standard (NQS) over the past decade. The analysis is based on data sourced from the Australian Childrens’ Education & Care Quality Authority’s (ACECQA) National Quality Standard datasets.

This analysis is broken into two sections: service quality and service count.

Service Quality

Almost 90% of services are meeting or exceeding the NQS, with sessional services exceeding LDC by 8% pt.

Change in this figure remains low, sitting around +1% YoY for Q4 2025.

Most services that are not meeting the NQS are either for-profit or on-site (state school) services.

Non-profits tend to have a higher proportion of services that exceed the NQS, especially for co-located services (state and independet).@cr-plot-rating-dist-highlighted

This figure compares overall NQS ratings against the SEIFA decile, which measures socioeconomic advantage and disadvantage of an area. The SEIFA metric is broken into ten groups, referred to as deciles, with 1 representing most disadvantage.

Areas with higher incidence of disadvantage have more services that do not meeting the NQS.

The inverse relationship is true for services exceeding the NQS.

For ‘Excellent’ rated services, we see a two-peak distribution for the most advantaged and the least advantaged areas. For the least advantaged areas, this may indicate how needs-based funding, such as SRF, is being used effectively by some services to deliver high quality education and care.

Service Count and Types

Growth in the number of services for the entire ECEC sector remains stead at around 1.5% YoY.

For kindergarten services (sessional and long day care), growth in the number of services is higher, but remains steady at 3% YoY.

For-profit operators remain the largest type of service provider, with this composition profile having remained almost the same over the past decade.

Sesssional providers are typically more likely to be non-profit, while long day care providers are more likely to be for-profit.
